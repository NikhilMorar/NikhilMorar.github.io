London's 1854 Cholera Epidemic


The Broad Street cholera outbreak was a severe outbreak of cholera in 1854 near Broad Street in the Soho district of the City of Westminster, London, England, and occurred during the 1846â€“1860 cholera pandemic happening worldwide. This outbreak, which killed 616 people, is best known for the physician John Snow's study of its causes and his hypothesis that germ-contaminated water was the source of cholera, rather than particles in the air. Snow later used a dot map to illustrate how cases of cholera occurred around this pump. Snow's efforts to connect cholera incidence with potential geographic sources were based on creating what is now known as a Voronoi diagram. He mapped the locations of individual water pumps and generated cells that represented all the points on his map closest to each pump. The section of Snow's map representing areas in the city where the closest available water source was the Broad Street pump included the highest incidence of cholera cases. (via Wikipedia)

This project was based on the original map, by John Snow, to show the clusters of cholera deaths (indicated by blue circles) and water pumps in the area (indicated by black squares). When viewing this visual, there is a strong correlation between cholera deaths to a water pump on Broad Street.
